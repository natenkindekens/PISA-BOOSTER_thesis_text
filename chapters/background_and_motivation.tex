\chapter{Background and Motivation: The State of Arithmetic Reserach Tools}


\section{Arithmetic research in primary school children: an active field}

Most people learn to count before they learn to read, and few skills follow a child further through life than the ability to do arithmetic. Yet despite its seemingly basic nature, arithmetic in primary school children remains one of the most actively studied topics in educational psychology. The reason for this is simple: we still do not fully understand how children learn it, why some struggle so much more than others, and what we can do about it.\\ \\
Part of what keeps this field alive is that the numbers coming out of large-scale assessments keep raising questions. Belgium's average PISA mathematics score dropped from 508 in 2018 to 489 in 2022. That is the lowest value recorded since the country began participating \cite{globaleconomy2022belgium}. This is not an isolated story either. PISA trend analysis shows that Belgium's decline in mathematics had already begun before 2018 (see Figure \ref{fig:pisa_scores_belgium}), meaning the drop cannot simply be blamed on the pandemic. The same pattern plays out internationally: across OECD countries, mean performance in mathematics fell by a record of 15 points between 2018 and 2022 \cite{oecd2023pisa}. Something is going wrong, and it is going wrong early.\\ \\
The stakes are higher than just test scores. High-quality mathematics education not only improves life outcomes for individuals but also drives innovation and progress across society \cite{ansari2025arithmetic}. McNeil et al. shows that the arithmetic skills a child builds in primary school are not just useful for that year, but they form the foundation for everything that comes after \cite{ansari2025arithmetic}. Early numeracy skills are robust predictors of later mathematical achievement, with strong correlations observed between early assessments and standardized test scores in later years. Children who fall behind in arithmetic early tend to stay behind, and catching up becomes progressively harder as the curriculum moves on. \\ \\ \begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{figures/pisa_scores_belgium.png}
  \caption[PISA math scores from ]
  {Evolution of Belgium's PISA mathematics scores from 2003 to 2022, showing a clear downward trend in average performance over time. \cite{globaleconomy2022belgium}}
  \label{fig:pisa_scores_belgium}
\end{figure} \\ \\
What makes this field particularly active is that arithmetic turns out to be far 
more complex than it looks from the outside. On the surface, solving a sum seems 
straightforward. But decades of research have made clear that children bring a 
whole collection of cognitive skills to the table when they do arithmetic. Skills that are still developing throughout primary school and that differ 
substantially from one child to the next. Some of these are specific to numbers: 
how well children process numerical quantities, or how quickly they retrieve 
arithmetic facts from memory. Others are more general: how well they can hold 
information in mind, how flexibly they switch between strategies, and perhaps 
most interestingly, how accurately they judge whether their own answers are 
correct. De Smedt \cite{desmedt2022} provides an overview of this 
landscape, noting that individual differences in mathematical performance are 
remarkably stable and already visible before children enter formal education. 
Understanding which factors drive these differences, and how they interact across 
development, remains an open and active research question. Researchers at KU 
Leuven have been prominent contributors to this effort: work by Bellon, Fias, and 
De Smedt has shed light on the role of metacognitive monitoring in arithmetic 
achievement \cite{bellon_more_2019, bellon2020, bellon2021} while Vanbinst and De Smedt 
have investigated how individual differences in numerical magnitude processing 
relate to arithmetic development \cite{vanbinst2016}. These are not isolated 
efforts. Similar questions are being pursued by research groups across the 
United Kingdom, Canada, the United States, and the rest of Europe, each 
approaching the problem from a different angle. \\ \\
This scope of ongoing research is itself the strongest argument for why good 
tools matter. Every study in this field depends on being able to observe 
children doing arithmetic under controlled conditions. Measuring not just 
whether they get the right answer, but how long it takes, how confident they are, 
and how their performance evolves over multiple sessions. The quality of that data 
depends directly on the quality of the tools used to collect it. As this thesis 
will argue, the tools currently available leave a significant gap between what 
researchers need and what they can actually get. That gap is the starting point 
for everything that follows.


\section{What a good research tool needs}

Before looking at what is currently available, it is worth being precise about 
what we are actually looking for. The previous section established that 
arithmetic research in primary school children is an active field with real 
scientific stakes. But good research questions can only produce good answers 
if the tools used to collect the data are up to the job. This section defines 
what that means in practice.

The six properties described below were developed through a combination of 
discussions with researchers in the field and a close reading of how existing 
tools are evaluated in the literature on experimental software for educational 
psychology \cite{peirce_psychopypsychophysics_2007,mathot_opensesame_2012}. They are not 
claimed to be exhaustive, but together they capture the core requirements 
that a tool must meet if it is to support rigorous, classroom-based research 
with children. Each property is introduced and briefly motivated here; the 
question of how well existing tools satisfy them is taken up in the following 
section.

\subsection*{Data Tracking}
The most basic requirement of any research tool is that it records what 
happens. In arithmetic research this means logging not just whether a child 
answered correctly, but also the response time, the exact answer given, the 
confidence rating if applicable, and the precise timestamp of each event. 
Without clean, structured, and complete data, no analysis is possible. This 
sounds obvious, but in practice many tools either do not log enough detail, 
make the export process cumbersome, or produce formats that require extensive 
preprocessing before they can be used. A good research tool must make data 
collection automatic, complete, and straightforward to access.


\subsection*{Experimental control}
Research designs in educational psychology typically involve comparing groups 
under different conditions. For example, children who receive metacognitive 
prompts versus those who do not. To draw valid conclusions from such a 
comparison, the researcher must be able to control precisely what each group 
sees and experiences. This includes controlling the type of exercises 
presented, the order in which they appear, which items are excluded, whether adaptive difficulty is active or not, and so on. Without this level of control, 
confounding variables (an outside, often hidden, third variable) creep in and the internal validity of the study is 
compromised \cite{shadish2002experimental}.

\subsection*{Reproducibility}
A finding is only scientifically meaningful if it can be replicated. For a 
tool to support reproducible research, it must be possible to save the 
complete configuration of an experiment --- every setting, every parameter, 
every exclusion rule --- and to share that configuration with other 
researchers so they can run the exact same study. This is closely tied to 
broader discussions in the open science community about transparency and 
replication, and it is a property that is surprisingly difficult to satisfy 
in practice: many existing tools rely on custom scripts or local configuration 
files that are hard to version, share, or reconstruct after the fact.

\subsection*{Flexibility}
A research tool is rarely used for a single study. Research groups run experiments over years, test new hypotheses, and adapt existing paradigms to new age groups or populations. What worked as a one-off protocol for a study on metacognitive monitoring may need to be reconfigured two years later with a different task, a new control condition, or an extended session structure. A good research tool must therefore make it possible to run not just one experiment, but many. And to modify or extend an existing setup without having to rebuild the tool from scratch.\\ \\
In practice, this means that the parameters a researcher is likely to vary between studies need to live outside the source code. The type of exercises, the number of trials, the timing windows, the inclusion of metacognitive prompts, the adaptive difficulty model, and the exclusion rules should all be configuration options, not hard-coded assumptions. If these aspects are built directly into the code, it does not just make things inconvenient. In some cases, it can make it impossible to set up the experiment in the way the researcher needs. This turns hard-coded choices into real limitations on the study design. That is why it is essential to keep these elements flexible and configurable.\\ \\

\subsection*{Reliability}
Reliability is the property that is easiest to take for granted and most damaging to lose. A research tool is reliable when it behaves the same way for every participant, every session, every time. Timings are accurate, inputs are captured without loss, the interface does not stutter at unpredictable moments, and a crash during session three of a four-session study does not destroy the data from sessions one and two. None of this is optional. The moment a tool becomes unreliable, the data it produces becomes hard to interpret: was that slow response time a real cognitive effect, or was the screen just slow to refresh that trial?\\ \\
The concern is not theoretical. Millisecond-scale timing errors in commonly used experimental equipment have been documented as a plausible contributor to replication failures in reaction-time research \cite{plant2013millisecond}. In an arithmetic study where the difference between a retrieved arithmetic fact and a calculated answer can be a few hundred milliseconds, a tool whose timing drifts is a tool that can no longer distinguish the effects it is meant to measure.\\ \\
Reliability also has a second dimension: the tool should simply keep working across the full duration of a study. Classroom research typically involves multiple sessions spread over days or weeks, running on hardware the researcher does not control — the school's laptops, the tablets in the library, a mix of browsers on a mix of operating systems. A tool that works perfectly on the researcher's development machine but fails intermittently in the wild is, for practical purposes, unreliable. A good research tool must therefore be both precise (its measurements are trustworthy) and robust (it keeps running under conditions that are not ideal).

\subsection*{Ease of setup}
The final property follows directly from the previous five. A tool can log every relevant event, offer complete experimental control, support reproducibility, allow flexible reconfiguration, and run reliably — and still be unusable if setting it up takes three weeks and a degree in computer science. The researchers who drive the field are, with few exceptions, not software engineers. If the tool they are meant to use assumes otherwise, there are really only two outcomes: either studies get delayed while someone else writes the experiment, or the study gets reshaped to fit what is easy to implement rather than what the research question actually requires.\\ \\
But ease of setup is more than the absence of programming. It includes the whole experience of getting from "I want to run this study" to "the first child is doing the first exercise." Can the researcher tell, by looking at the interface, what each setting does? Is the navigation between configuration screens clear enough that a teacher could manage it during a short training session? When something is misconfigured, does the tool flag the problem before a classroom full of children is already logged in? These are standard concerns in user interface design, and cognitive load theory in particular has shown that interfaces which impose high extraneous load — clutter, ambiguous controls, inconsistent layout — measurably degrade the user's ability to complete their task \cite{darejeh2024cognitive}. For a research tool, that degradation translates directly into misconfigured studies and wasted sessions.\\ \\
A good research tool must therefore be designed for the people who will actually use it. That means visual clarity, predictable behaviour, sensible defaults, and enough guidance built into the interface that a researcher or teacher can configure a study without needing a separate manual open on the side.

\section{Current research tools and their limitations}

With a clear picture of what a good research tool needs to do, the next step is to look at what researchers actually have on their desks today. Several tools have become standard in the educational psychology community, and most arithmetic studies published in the last decade have used one of them. This section walks through the two most prominent — OpenSesame \cite{mathot_opensesame_2012} and PsychoPy \cite{peirce2019psychopy2} — and examines how each of them fares against the six properties introduced in Section 2.2. Both tools have been carefully designed, are actively maintained, and have earned their place in the field. The point of this section is not to argue that they are bad tools; it is to show that even the best tools currently available leave a specific kind of gap, and that this gap becomes critical the moment a study leaves the controlled lab and moves into a classroom.\\ \\
The discussion starts with OpenSesame, then turns to PsychoPy, briefly acknowledges the broader landscape of alternatives, and ends by drawing out the limitations that all of these tools share. That final discussion is what motivates the shift in direction taken in Chapter 3.

\subsection{OpenSesame}
OpenSesame is an open-source graphical experiment builder developed by Mathôt, Schreij, and Theeuwes \cite{mathot2012opensesame}. It was designed to make the construction of experiments in the social sciences more accessible than before by providing a graphical user interface (GUI) on top of a Python back-end. A researcher assembles an experiment by dragging items — trial loops, sketchpads, keyboard response components, inline scripts — into a tree structure that represents the flow of the study (as shown in Figure \ref{fig:opensesame_gui}). For anything the GUI does not cover, OpenSesame exposes an inline Python editor that lets the researcher drop directly into code \cite{mathot2012opensesame}. It is cross-platform, free, and has become one of the most widely used tools in experimental psychology.\\ \\
Against the six properties from Section 2.2, OpenSesame scores well on five of them. \textbf{Data tracking} is handled automatically: every trial produces a row in a structured log file, with response times, accuracy, and any custom variables the experimenter defines. \textbf{Experimental control} is one of its strongest points — the researcher has essentially full authority over what each participant sees, the order of stimuli, the exact timing of each event, and the conditions under which the experiment branches. \textbf{Reproducibility} is built into the format: an OpenSesame experiment is stored as a single file that bundles the configuration, the stimuli, and any inline scripts, which means that sharing the experiment with a collaborator is as simple as sharing that file. \textbf{Flexibility} is high because the inline Python escape hatch means there is almost no experimental design the tool cannot, in principle, express. And \textbf{reliability}, in the timing-precision sense, is solid: OpenSesame can delegate stimulus presentation to proven back-ends such as PsychoPy to keep timing accurate at the millisecond level.\\ \\
The property where OpenSesame runs into trouble is \textbf{ease of setup}. On the surface, the GUI looks approachable: drag some items, connect them, press run. But the moment the study steps outside the standard stimulus-response template, the researcher is pulled into Python. Adding a custom adaptive algorithm, implementing a non-trivial exclusion rule, conditionally showing feedback based on the previous answer, or linking trial sequences across multiple sessions. All of these require inline scripts. These scripts are not decoration; they become critical components of the experiment, and maintaining them demands real programming skill. A researcher without a background in Python is effectively stuck with what the GUI alone can do, which for anything beyond a classical stimulus-response paradigm is not enough.\\ \\

OpenSesame exposes a large number of configuration options across several panels and tabs, many of which have defaults that only make sense once you already understand the underlying experiment-building model. The vocabulary is technical — loops, sketchpads, synchronisation back-ends — and the relationship between these items is not visually obvious until the researcher has spent time reading the documentation and working through the tutorial. For an experienced user, this is manageable. For a teacher or clinician who just wants to run a short study in a classroom, the learning curve is steep enough that the tool is effectively inaccessible without a trained research assistant in the room.\\ \\
The result is a tool that is genuinely well-built for the population it was designed for — experimental psychologists with at least some programming exposure — but that draws a hard line between "what the GUI can do" and "what the study actually needs." Most real arithmetic studies sit on the wrong side of that line.

\subsection{PsychoPy}
PsychoPy is the second major open-source tool in this space. Originally developed by Peirce as a Python library for psychophysics experiments \cite{peirce_psychopypsychophysics_2007}, it has since grown into a full experiment-building environment with its own graphical Builder interface \cite{peirce2019psychopy2}. Where OpenSesame started from the GUI and added Python as an escape hatch, PsychoPy came from the opposite direction: it began as a pure coding library and later wrapped a graphical interface around it. This difference in origin is important, because it affects how the tool behaves from a user's perspective.\\ \\
On the five research-oriented properties, PsychoPy performs at least as well as OpenSesame, and in some areas arguably better. \textbf{Data tracking} is thorough: PsychoPy logs every trial automatically, and because the underlying data structures are standard Python objects, researchers can extend the logging with custom computed fields without much effort. \textbf{Experimental control} is a core strength. The researcher has full authority over what appears on screen, when it appears, how long it stays, and what happens next — down to the level of individual frames if needed \cite{peirce2019psychopy2}. \textbf{Reproducibility} is supported by the fact that Builder experiments can be saved and shared as structured files, and the platform has explicitly invested in open-science features such as integration with the Open Science Framework. \textbf{Flexibility} is, on paper, the highest of any tool discussed here: since PsychoPy is fundamentally a Python library, a researcher who is willing to write code can implement essentially any experimental design. \textbf{Reliability} is another area where PsychoPy stands out. It was originally developed precisely because existing tools did not offer the timing accuracy that psychophysics research demands \cite{peirce_psychopypsychophysics_2007}, and its timing precision remains one of its defining strengths. On the robustness side, PsychoPy is a mature and actively maintained project with a large user community, which means that bugs tend to be caught and fixed relatively quickly — though, like OpenSesame, it was designed for controlled lab environments rather than for the unpredictable hardware mix of a primary school classroom.\\ \\
That leaves \textbf{ease of setup}, and here PsychoPy faces the same fundamental challenge as OpenSesame, but arguably in a more pronounced form. The graphical Builder interface offers a drag-and-drop workflow that works well for simple experiments. But PsychoPy's own documentation is explicit about the trade-off: the Builder covers standard cases, and for anything more complex the researcher is expected to write raw Python code \cite{peirce2019psychopy2}. This is also a one-way process — once the generated code has been edited by hand, it cannot be converted back into the graphical view. At that point the experiment is no longer something you configure through an interface; it is a Python project that has to be maintained as one.
The interface itself reinforces this impression. Even before touching any code, a first-time user is confronted with terminology like Routines, Components, Backends, and Monitor configurations — concepts that make sense to someone with a programming background but that are far from self-evident to a teacher or educational researcher encountering the tool for the first time. Where OpenSesame at least attempts to hide the Python layer behind a familiar drag-and-drop tree, PsychoPy keeps it closer to the surface. Figure~\ref{fig:psychopy-ui} illustrates this well: the interface is undeniably capable, but understanding what it is showing you already requires a significant amount of prior knowledge.\\ \\

\subsection{Other tools in the landscape}

\subsection{Shared limitations}
The previous sections have shown that OpenSesame and PsychoPy are strong research tools. On five of the six properties defined in Section 2.2 they deliver exactly what a researcher needs: clean data tracking, full experimental control, reproducible setups, flexible designs, and reliability. But the picture changes completely when you step back and ask a different question: what is it actually like for a child to sit in front of one of these tools?\\ \\
The answer, in most cases, is that it is not a pleasant experience. A typical arithmetic experiment built in OpenSesame or PsychoPy presents the child with white text on a black screen, one problem at a time, with no story, no colour, no reward, and no sense of progress (see example Figure \ref{fig:black_screen}). There is no character guiding them through the session, no puzzle to unlock after ten correct answers, no way to choose what kind of exercise they want to do next. The experiment simply moves from one trial to the next until it is done. For a single short session in a quiet lab, this can work. But arithmetic research increasingly takes place in classrooms, across multiple sessions spread over days or weeks, with children who are seven or eight years old. In that context, a black screen with numbers on it is not enough.\\ \\
This is not an oversight by the developers of these tools. OpenSesame and PsychoPy were built to be general-purpose experiment builders, not arithmetic learning environments. They were designed to give the researcher maximum control over stimuli and timing, and they do that well. But the consequence is that anything beyond the bare stimulus-response loop — gamification, a narrative wrapper, adaptive difficulty, visual feedback, a child-friendly login system, etc — has to be built from scratch by the researcher, in Python. And that is where the problem becomes real. The researchers who need these features are educational psychologists, not software engineers. They know exactly what kind of experience they want the child to have: exercises that adapt to the child's level, a colourful interface that feels like a game, metacognitive prompts wrapped in a story rather than presented as a clinical question. But translating that vision into working code is a skill most of them do not have, and hiring or collaborating with a developer for every study is neither practical nor scalable.\\ \\

\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{figures/zwart_scherm.png}
\caption{Example of a stimulus presentation screen created in OpenSesame, showing a simple arithmetic task with multiple-choice response options.}
\label{fig:black_screen}
\end{figure}This creates a frustrating situation. Researchers who study arithmetic learning in children know from their own literature that engagement matters, that motivation declines over repeated sessions if the task feels repetitive, and that adaptive difficulty helps keep children in a productive learning zone. They also know that personalisation — letting a child choose their own difficulty, see their own progress, or pick which exercise to do next — can increase ownership and metacognitive awareness. These are not luxuries; they are features that directly affect the quality of the data a study produces. A child who is bored or frustrated is not giving the researcher a reliable window into their arithmetic ability. Yet the tools these researchers have at their disposal make it extremely difficult to include any of these features without significant programming effort.\\ \\
The result is a kind of forced compromise. Researchers end up running studies with interfaces that look nothing like what children are used to in their daily digital lives, in environments that strip away every motivational element, using tools that children have never seen before and will never see again. The experiment measures arithmetic performance, but it does so in conditions that are far removed from the classroom context the research is ultimately trying to understand. Whether this matters, and how much it matters, is the question taken up in Section 2.4.

\section{Why this matters: the cost of a bad research tool}
The limitations described in Section 2.3 are not just inconveniences for the researcher setting up a study. They have direct consequences for the quality of the research itself. When the tool a child interacts with during an experiment is unfamiliar, visually unappealing, and lacking in motivational features, the data that comes out of that experiment is affected in ways that are hard to control for after the fact. This section discusses three mechanisms through which a poor research tool can compromise a study: reduced ecological validity, motivation drop across sessions, and the confounding effect of tool unfamiliarity.

\subsection*{Ecological validity}
Ecological validity refers to how well the conditions of a study match the real-world context the research aims to understand \cite{bronfenbrenner1977toward}. Bronfenbrenner argued that studies conducted in artificial laboratory settings, which differ from children’s everyday environments, often produce results that are difficult to generalise. Instead, he emphasised the importance of conducting research in natural settings where learning actually occurs, even if these settings are more complex \cite{bronfenbrenner1977toward}.\\ \\
Internal validity refers to the extent to which a study can establish a reliable cause-and-effect relationship, while external validity concerns the extent to which findings can be generalised beyond the study context \cite{shadish2002experimental}. Shadish, Cook, and Campbell make a similar point in the context of experimental design more broadly: highly controlled or manipulated research environments may improve internal validity, but they do so at the cost of external validity, because the conditions no longer reflect the dynamic and adaptive nature of real-world teaching and learning \cite{shadish2002experimental}.

\subsection*{Motivation drop}
The second cost is more practical and arguably more damaging: children lose motivation. Research in arithmetic development increasingly relies on multi-session designs, where children are tested across several days or weeks to track learning trajectories, strategy shifts, or the effects of an intervention over time. This only works if the children remain engaged throughout. A study by Sievertsen, Gino, and Piovesan on cognitive fatigue in children taking standardised tests found that performance declined measurably as a function of time spent on cognitively demanding tasks, and that breaks and changes in activity could partially restore performance \cite{sievertsen2016cognitive}. Their findings suggest that sustained mental effort without motivational support leads not just to subjective tiredness, but to objectively worse outcomes.\\ \\
When the tool itself offers no variation, no rewards, no story, and no sense of progress, the motivation problem is amplified. Research on the role of motivation in mental fatigue has shown that participants who receive motivational incentives are able to maintain performance levels on demanding tasks over extended periods, while those in non-rewarded conditions show clear performance declines \cite{hopstaken2015role}. In the context of arithmetic research with children, this translates to a concrete risk: if the experiment feels boring by session two, the data from sessions three and four may reflect disengagement rather than actual arithmetic ability. The researcher ends up measuring motivation, not mathematics.\\ \\
Systematic reviews of gamification in mathematics education consistently report that game elements such as points, levels, narrative framing, adaptive difficulty, and immediate feedback can sustain engagement and motivation over time \cite{manzanoleon2021gamification}. These are precisely the features that current research tools lack and that the programming barrier described in Section 2.3 makes difficult to add.

\subsection*{The familiarity effect}
The third mechanism is subtler but no less important. When a child encounters a completely unfamiliar tool during a research session, the tool itself becomes a variable in the experiment. The child has to figure out where to type an answer, what the buttons do, how the feedback works, and what is expected of them, all while supposedly demonstrating their arithmetic ability. Fuchs and Fuchs demonstrated through a meta-analysis of 22 controlled studies that familiarity with the testing context raises children's test performance by an average of 0.28 standard deviations \cite{fuchs1986test}. This is not a small effect. It means that unfamiliarity with the testing tool leads to a consistent decrease in performance.\\ \\
If the research tool were also used outside of the experiment, for regular classroom practice or homework, children would arrive at the experimental session already knowing how the interface works, what the buttons do, and what to expect. The tool would be invisible in the way that a pencil is invisible during a written test: present, but not a source of confusion. This familiarity would reduce irrelevant cognitive load during the experiment and make the data a cleaner reflection of what the study is actually trying to measure. It would also open up a second advantage: discoveries made through research, for example that metacognitive prompts improve arithmetic learning, could be fed back into the same tool and reach children as part of their regular practice. The tool would not only serve research but also benefit from it.

\subsection*{The bottom line}
All three mechanisms point in the same direction. A research tool that is ecologically valid, motivationally engaging, and familiar to the children who use it is not a luxury or a nice-to-have. It is a prerequisite for producing data that accurately reflects what it claims to measure. In short: a better tool means better research. The question, then, is where to find such a tool. The natural place to look is at the platforms children already use for arithmetic practice in classrooms. That is the direction taken in Chapter 3.
