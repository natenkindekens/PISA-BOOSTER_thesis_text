\chapter{Exercise Tools as a Solution Direction}
The previous chapter established that the tools currently used for arithmetic research do their job as research instruments but fail as experiences for the children who use them. They lack the motivational, adaptive, and visual qualities that keep children engaged across multiple sessions, and the programming barrier makes it impractical for researchers to add these qualities themselves. The question that follows naturally is: where do these qualities already exist?\\ \\
The answer is not far away. Across primary schools in Belgium and the Netherlands, children already use digital tools for arithmetic practice on a daily basis. Platforms like Automatus \cite{automatus} and Rekentuin \cite{rekentuin} are designed from the ground up to be engaging, adaptive, and easy for children to use. They look like games, they feel like games, and children are willing to spend time on them without being asked twice. In other words, these tools already solve many of the problems identified in Section 2.4: they offer the ecological validity, the sustained motivation, and the familiarity that research tools lack.\\ \\
This chapter examines exercise tools as a possible solution direction. It begins by defining what makes a good exercise tool, then walks through two prominent examples in the field, and ends by asking the inevitable follow-up question: if exercise tools solve the engagement problem, can they also serve as research tools? The answer, as will become clear, is not quite.

\section{What a good exercise tool needs}
Where Section 2.2 asked what a research tool needs, this section asks the same question for the other side: what makes a good tool for arithmetic practice in the classroom? The properties listed below were developed through discussions with primary school teachers, educational researchers, and the co-supervisor of this thesis, and reflect what practitioners in the field consistently identify as important. Where possible, connections to the educational psychology literature are drawn to ground these observations in theory.

\subsection*{Adaptivity}
Perhaps the single most important property of a good exercise tool is that it adapts to the child using it. Not every child in a classroom is at the same level, and a tool that presents the same exercises to everyone will inevitably bore the strongest students and frustrate the weakest ones. This idea has deep theoretical roots. Vygotsky's concept of the zone of proximal development describes the range of tasks that a child cannot yet do independently but can accomplish with appropriate support \cite{vygotsky1978mind}. A good adaptive system operationalises this concept by selecting exercises that sit within the child's zone: challenging enough to promote learning, but not so hard that the child gives up. Csikszentmihalyi's flow theory makes a closely related point from the perspective of engagement: when the difficulty of a task is well-matched to the learner's skill level, the learner enters a state of deep concentration and intrinsic motivation; when the balance is off, the result is either anxiety or boredom \cite{csikszentmihalyi1990flow}. An exercise tool that does not adapt is, in effect, guaranteeing that most of the children using it will be outside their flow channel most of the time.

\subsection*{Engagement}
Adaptivity keeps the difficulty right, but it does not on its own make a child want to open the tool again tomorrow. Engagement is about everything else that makes the experience feel worthwhile: a story that gives context to the exercises, visual rewards for progress, puzzles to unlock, a character to interact with. These are gamification elements, and while the term is sometimes used loosely, systematic reviews of gamification in education have consistently found that elements such as points, levels, narrative framing, and immediate feedback can improve motivation and engagement in mathematics \cite{manzanoleon2021gamification}. For an exercise tool aimed at primary school children, engagement is not optional. A tool that children do not want to use is a tool that does not get used, regardless of how well-designed its exercises are.

\subsection*{Feedback}
Children need to know whether they got the answer right, and ideally they need to know it immediately. Delayed or absent feedback leaves the child uncertain about whether they are learning, and it removes one of the most natural drivers of improvement: the desire to correct a mistake you just made. Good feedback goes beyond a simple correct/incorrect signal. It can show the right answer when the child was wrong, acknowledge a good self-assessment when the child judged their own confidence accurately, or offer encouragement after a difficult exercise. The key is that the feedback is timely, clear, and meaningful.

\subsection*{Personalisation}
Personalisation is related to adaptivity but distinct from it. Where adaptivity adjusts the difficulty automatically based on performance, personalisation gives the child the feeling that the tool is theirs. This has two sides. The first is functional: letting children choose which type of exercise to practise, whether they want to see a timer, or what difficulty level they prefer. This kind of choice can foster a sense of ownership and metacognitive awareness. For example, when a child decides to practise multiplication because they know it is their weakest area, they are doing something more valuable than just answering another question. The second side is personal identity: an avatar, a nickname, a progress tracker that shows their journey, a customised home screen. These elements may seem cosmetic, but they transform a generic program into something that feels like it belongs to the child, which in turn makes the child more likely to come back to it.

\subsection*{Accessibility}
An exercise tool is only useful if children can actually reach it. In practice this means the tool should run in a web browser, work on the devices that schools actually have (laptops, tablets, Chromebooks), and not require any software installation. It also means the tool should be available outside the classroom, so that children can practise at home if they want to. A tool that only works on a specific operating system, or that requires a local installation managed by an IT department, will inevitably reach fewer children than one that runs on any device with a browser.

\subsection*{User friendliness}
Finally, the tool has to be usable by its target audience, which in this case is children between roughly seven and eleven years old. This means large buttons, clear icons, simple navigation, and a visual language that children understand without needing instructions. Cognitive load theory reminds us that every unnecessary interface element competes for the limited working memory resources that the child should be spending on the actual exercise \cite{sweller1988cognitive}. A well-designed exercise tool is one where the interface disappears: the child thinks about the arithmetic, not about how to operate the tool.

\section{Existing execise tools: Automatus and Rekentuin}

\subsection{Automatus}
Automatus is a web-based arithmetic practice platform widely used in primary schools across Flanders and the Netherlands. It was designed with a clear purpose: to help children automate their arithmetic skills through repeated, structured practice. The platform organises exercises into a grid of tiles arranged on a map, where each tile corresponds to a specific type of problem (for example, additions up to 20, or multiplication tables of 7). Teachers can create customised "lands", collections of tiles that form a visual path children work through, giving the practice sessions a sense of direction and progression. Figure~\ref{fig:automatus_ui} shows the main interface.
\begin{figure}[ht]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{figures/automatus_ui.png}
\caption{Screenshot of the Automatus interface from the pupil's perspective, showing the tile-based landscape through which learners navigate exercises.}
\label{fig:automatus_ui}
\end{figure}\\ \\
On the six exercise-tool properties from Section 3.1, Automatus scores remarkably well on five of them. \textbf{Adaptivity} is built into the core of the platform: the system tracks the child's performance and adjusts which exercises are presented based on where mistakes occur, ensuring that children spend more time on the areas where they need it most. \textbf{Engagement} is supported through the map-and-tile structure, which gives children a visible sense of progress as they work through a land. The platform also tracks response times and lets children compare their current performance against their personal best and their own average, which adds a layer of self-competition that keeps practice sessions from feeling repetitive. \textbf{Feedback} is one of Automatus's strongest points: when a child answers incorrectly, the application pauses the exercise progress and shows an explanation that walks through the correct approach. \textbf{Accessibility} is excellent: Automatus runs as a website in any browser and is also available as a tablet application, which means it works on the devices schools and families actually have. \textbf{User friendliness} is equally strong: the interface is clean, visually appealing, and intuitive enough that children can navigate it without adult help.\\ \\
The one property where Automatus does not fully deliver is \textbf{personalisation}. Children can in some cases choose which exercises they want to work on, and the performance tracking gives a sense of individual progress. But there is no avatar, no personal profile beyond a name, no visual element that makes the tool feel like it belongs to the child rather than to the classroom. This is a minor gap in an otherwise very well-designed tool.

\subsection{Rekentuin}
Rekentuin (Math Garden) is a web-based arithmetic learning environment developed at the University of Amsterdam by Klinkenberg, Straatemeier, and van der Maas \cite{klinkenberg2011computer}. Originally built as both a scientific instrument and a practice platform, it has grown into one of the most widely used arithmetic tools in the Netherlands, serving over 400,000 primary school children. It is now maintained and distributed as part of the Prowise Learn ecosystem.\\ \\
The platform organises its content around a world map with multiple thematic islands, each representing a different mathematical domain. Children navigate between islands, discover new games, and work through exercises that range from basic addition to fractions and percentages. Each building or location on an island corresponds to a specific game, giving the exploration a sense of discovery and variety that keeps the experience from feeling repetitive. Figure~\ref{fig:rekentuin_ui} shows the main interface.
\begin{figure}[ht]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{figures/Rekentuin.png}
\caption{Screenshot of the Rekentuin interface from the pupil's perspective, showing the different continents from which learners can select different exercises.}
\label{fig:rekentuin_ui}
\end{figure}\\ \\
On the six properties, Rekentuin delivers strongly across the board. \textbf{Adaptivity} is the cornerstone of the platform's design. The system uses an item response model based on the Elo rating system to estimate both the child's ability and the difficulty of each item in real time, updating both estimates after every single answer \cite{klinkenberg2011computer}. Items are selected so that the child has roughly a 75\% probability of answering correctly. A balance point that keeps the task challenging without being discouraging. Children can also choose between three difficulty levels within each game, adding a layer of self-regulation on top of the adaptive algorithm. \textbf{Engagement} is sustained through the island-based world, a variety of games across different mathematical topics, and a coin system: faster and more accurate answers earn more coins. The platform also highlights recommended games and "super games" based on the child's performance, gently steering practice toward areas that need attention without making it feel like a punishment. \textbf{Feedback} is immediate. Children see whether their answer was correct after each problem, and the coin reward provides a secondary signal of how well they performed relative to the time limit. \textbf{Personalisation} is where Rekentuin distinguishes itself most clearly from Automatus. The coins children earn are directly tied to an avatar that they can dress, decorate, and customise. This gives every child a visible, personal identity within the platform. Combined with the personalised game recommendations and the self-selected difficulty levels, the experience feels suited to each individual. \textbf{Accessibility} is strong: Rekentuin runs in a web browser on any device, and data from Klinkenberg et al. shows that children completed roughly a third of their exercises outside school hours \cite{klinkenberg2011computer}, which indicates that the platform is genuinely used at home as well as in the classroom. \textbf{User friendliness} is well-designed for the target age group, with large interactive elements, colourful visuals, and a navigation structure built around the world and islands that children understand intuitively.\\ \\
At this point, a similar comparison to the one drawn for OpenSesame and PsychoPy in Section 2.3 is useful. Figure~\ref{fig:exercise-tool-grid} evaluates both Automatus and Rekentuin against the six exercise-tool properties from Section 3.1. The picture is almost the mirror image of the research-tool grid: where OpenSesame and PsychoPy scored well on the research properties but stumbled on ease of setup, Automatus and Rekentuin score well on nearly all exercise-tool properties. Automatus falls just short on personalisation, while Rekentuin delivers on all six. These are strong tools that do exactly what they were designed to do. \section{Why exercise tools alone are not the answer}
The previous section showed that Automatus and Rekentuin are excellent exercise tools. They are adaptive, engaging, well-designed, and genuinely enjoyed by children. A natural question follows: if these tools already solve the engagement and motivation problems that plague research tools, could they not simply be used for research as well?\\ \\
To answer this, the same six research-tool properties from Section 2.2 can be applied to both platforms. The result, summarised alongside the exercise-tool evaluation in Figure~\ref{fig:combined-grid}, reveals a clear pattern. On three of the six research properties, both tools perform well. On the other three, they fall short in ways that matter.\\ \\
The three properties that hold up are straightforward. \textbf{Reproducibility} is well-supported in both platforms: once a teacher or researcher has configured a set of exercises for a group of children, that configuration is saved and can be reused or shared. Running the same setup again next semester is not a problem. \textbf{Reliability} is equally strong. Both Automatus and Rekentuin are mature, commercially maintained products used daily by hundreds of thousands of children. They are stable, they do not crash mid-session, and they behave consistently across devices and browsers. \textbf{Ease of setup} is, unsurprisingly, a strength rather than a weakness. Both tools were built for teachers, not programmers. Setting up a class, assigning exercises, and managing student progress is done entirely through a graphical dashboard with no code involved.\\ \\
The problems emerge in the remaining three properties. \textbf{Data tracking} is the first issue. Both platforms collect data on student performance, and teachers can see summaries of accuracy and progress over time. But the level of detail is not what a researcher needs. Arithmetic research requires the exact response time for every individual trial, the precise answer the child gave (not just whether it was correct), the confidence judgement if one was elicited, and ideally the timestamp of every screen transition. These fine-grained data points are what allow researchers to distinguish, for example, between a child who retrieved a fact from memory in under two seconds and one who calculated the answer in five, a distinction that is central to understanding arithmetic development \cite{desmedt2022}. Exercise tools log enough to inform a teacher; they do not log enough to inform a study.\\ \\
\textbf{Experimental control} is the second and perhaps most fundamental problem. Research designs in educational psychology depend on the researcher being able to dictate exactly what each participant sees and under what conditions \cite{shadish2002experimental}. A study comparing children who receive metacognitive prompts to those who do not requires two groups with identical exercise sets but different feedback conditions. A study on the effect of adaptive difficulty needs the ability to switch adaptivity off for a control group while keeping everything else the same. Exercise tools are designed around the opposite principle: they are built to give children autonomy. The child chooses what to practise, the system adapts the difficulty on its own, and the teacher sets broad goals rather than trial-by-trial sequences. This is exactly what makes these tools good for learning, but it is also what makes them unsuitable for controlled experiments. There is no way to disable specific features for one group, no way to force a fixed sequence of problems, and no way to ensure that two children see exactly the same set of exercises.\\ \\
\textbf{Flexibility} is the third gap, and it is closely tied to the previous one. In a research context, flexibility means being able to set up a wide variety of experimental designs within the same tool. Varying the number of trials per block, the operations included, the exclusion rules for specific items, the presence or absence of a timer, and so on. Exercise tools offer some configuration options, but these are designed for pedagogical goals, not for experimental ones. A teacher can choose to focus a session on multiplication, but a researcher who needs precisely 30 addition problems followed by 20 subtraction problems with no repeated operands, no ties, and a metacognitive prompt after every fifth trial has no way to express that within these platforms. The kind of flexibility a programming language offers is absent, but more importantly, even within the graphical interface itself there is no mechanism for defining the structured, multi-block exercise sequences that most research designs require.\\ \\
Figure~\ref{fig:combined-grid} brings both evaluations together. The research-tool properties are on the left, the exercise-tool properties on the right. Automatus and Rekentuin fill the right side almost entirely with green. The left side tells a different story: reproducibility, reliability, and ease of setup are covered, but data tracking, experimental control, and flexibility are not. Exercise tools, on their own, are not the answer. But the qualities they bring to the table are exactly the qualities that Chapter 2 identified as missing from research tools. The next section brings all four tools and all twelve properties together to make this gap fully visible.

\section{The gap: no existing tool covers both sides}
The previous sections have built up the comparison piece by piece. Section 2.3 evaluated OpenSesame and PsychoPy on the research-tool properties. Section 3.2 evaluated Automatus and Rekentuin on the exercise-tool properties. Section 3.3 then tested whether the exercise tools could also serve as research tools, and found that they could not. The one combination that has not yet been made explicit is the reverse: how do the research tools score on the exercise-tool properties?\\ \\
Section 2.3.4 already answered this question in substance. OpenSesame and PsychoPy were not built to be exercise tools, and they offer none of the six properties from Section 3.1 out of the box. There is no built-in adaptivity, no gamification, no avatar system, no child-friendly login, no feedback beyond what the researcher programs manually. As discussed there, all of these features could in theory be implemented, but only by writing them from scratch in Python, which brings the researcher back to the same programming wall that already made ease of setup the weakest point on the research side.\\ \\
Figure~\ref{fig:full-grid} brings everything together. All four tools are evaluated against all twelve properties. The six research-tool properties from Section 2.2 on the left, the six exercise-tool properties from Section 3.1 on the right. The pattern is now fully visible. Automatus and Rekentuin fill the exercise columns with green but leave gaps in the research columns. OpenSesame and PsychoPy fill the research columns with green but leave the exercise columns empty. Each tool excels at what it was designed for. None covers both sides.

This is not a criticism of any of these tools individually. Each one was built for a specific purpose and serves that purpose well. But for a researcher who wants to run a controlled experiment in a classroom, with children who stay motivated across multiple sessions, using a tool that feels familiar rather than foreign, there is currently no option that works.
